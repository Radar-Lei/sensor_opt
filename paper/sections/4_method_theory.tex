\section{TRACE-SL method and scoped theory}
\label{sec:method}

TRACE-SL is a reconstruction-aware candidate-selection and refinement protocol. It is not a single closed-form score. The method is organized around three decisions that are common in deployment studies: which candidate layouts to consider, which score vector to trust on validation data, and whether a local exchange improves actual reconstruction rather than only a proxy certificate.

\subsection{Transparent reconstruction layer}

For a fixed layout $\calS$, the GLS/MAP reconstruction layer estimates training means and covariances and reconstructs hidden components from observed components. In the linear-Gaussian version used to motivate the certificates, let
\[
    x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma), \qquad
    y_{\calS}=H_{\calS}x+\varepsilon, \qquad
    \varepsilon\sim \mathcal{N}(0,R_{\calS}),
\]
where $H_{\calS}$ selects observed components and $R_{\calS}$ is observation noise. The posterior mean is the transparent reconstruction rule and the hidden posterior covariance is
\[
    \Sigma_{\calH|\calS}
    =
    \Sigma_{\calH\calH}
    -
    \Sigma_{\calH\calS}
    \left(\Sigma_{\calS\calS}+R_{\calS}\right)^{-1}
    \Sigma_{\calS\calH}.
\]
Graph-signal reconstruction uses the same layout interface but replaces the covariance-conditioned estimator with graph-regularized recovery. TRACE-SL can therefore compare covariance, graph, POD, and validation losses without changing the held-out metric.

The candidate pool is intentionally heterogeneous. Posterior A-trace and D-logdet represent classical uncertainty certificates; scenario-average and scenario-CVaR trace expose average-case and tail-risk variants; top variance and degree are simple reviewer-facing heuristics; graph sampling and QR/POD represent graph-signal and modal reconstruction baselines; random and validation-selected random isolate the value of validation selection from certificate-guided candidate generation. The final TRACE-SL layout is selected by reconstruction loss on validation data rather than by assuming that any single proxy score is universally correct.

\subsection{Formal scoped properties}

\begin{proposition}[Posterior trace identity]
\label{prop:trace}
Under the linear-Gaussian model above, the posterior mean $\E[x_{\calH}\mid y_{\calS}]$ minimizes conditional squared reconstruction error among measurable estimators of $x_{\calH}$, and
\[
    \E\!\left[\left\|x_{\calH}-\E[x_{\calH}\mid y_{\calS}]\right\|_2^2 \mid \calS\right]
    =\tr(\Sigma_{\calH|\calS}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For squared loss, the conditional expectation is the Bayes estimator. Conditional on $y_{\calS}$, the residual $x_{\calH}-\E[x_{\calH}\mid y_{\calS}]$ has covariance $\Sigma_{\calH|\calS}$. Taking the trace of the residual second moment gives the conditional expected squared Euclidean error. The statement is conditional on the fixed prior and observation model; it does not imply an MAE guarantee for non-Gaussian traffic data.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Posterior covariance monotonicity for fixed targets]
\label{prop:mono}
Fix a target set $\calQ\subseteq\calV$ whose state is to be reconstructed, and compare two observation sets $\calS\subseteq\calS'$ disjoint from $\calQ$ under the same prior and observation-noise model. Then
\[
    \Sigma_{\calQ|\calS'} \preceq \Sigma_{\calQ|\calS},
    \qquad
    \tr(\Sigma_{\calQ|\calS'}) \le \tr(\Sigma_{\calQ|\calS}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Conditioning on $\calS'$ is equivalent to conditioning first on $\calS$ and then on the additional observations $\calS'\setminus\calS$. The Gaussian conditioning update subtracts a positive semidefinite Schur-complement term from the previous posterior covariance. Trace monotonicity follows from the Loewner-order relation. The fixed-target condition is needed because the hidden complement changes when sensors are added.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Finite-candidate validation selection bound]
\label{prop:validation}
Let $\calC$ be a finite candidate-layout class evaluated on an independent validation split of size $n_{\mathrm{v}}$. Suppose the validation losses $\ell(x_{\calH},f_{\calS}(x_{\calS}))$ are bounded in $[0,B]$ for every $\calS\in\calC$. Then, for any $\epsilon>0$,
\[
    \Pr\!\left(
    \sup_{\calS\in\calC}
    \left|R(\calS)-\hat R_{\mathrm{v}}(\calS)\right|>\epsilon
    \right)
    \le
    2|\calC|\exp\!\left(-\frac{2n_{\mathrm{v}}\epsilon^2}{B^2}\right).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed layout, Hoeffding's inequality controls the deviation between validation loss and deployment risk. A union bound over the finite candidate class gives the displayed inequality. For sub-exponential traffic losses the same role is played by the corresponding Bernstein-type bound with different constants. The proposition supports validation-calibrated selection as a statistically meaningful model-selection step; it is not a test-set performance theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Finite termination and evaluated-neighborhood local optimality]
\label{prop:swap}
For fixed validation data, deterministic validation loss, finite add/remove pools, and finite iteration limits, Algorithm~\ref{alg:swap} terminates. If it stops because no evaluated remove/add exchange strictly improves validation loss, the returned layout is locally optimal with respect to the evaluated one-swap neighborhood.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The configured neighborhood is finite and each accepted step strictly decreases the deterministic validation loss among evaluated layouts. With finite iteration limits termination is immediate; without repeated strict improvements among a finite set also cannot continue indefinitely. At the no-improvement stopping condition, every evaluated one-swap neighbor has validation loss at least as large as the returned layout. The statement says nothing about unevaluated swaps, larger neighborhoods, or global optimality.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Complexity and non-claims]
The workload decomposes into candidate generation, candidate scoring, reconstruction evaluation, and swap evaluation. If $M$ candidates are evaluated, $|\Lambda|$ score vectors are tuned, $n_{\mathrm{v}}$ validation time points are used, and each reconstruction evaluation costs $C_{\mathrm{rec}}(k,|\calV|)$, the validation-scoring component scales as $O(M|\Lambda|n_{\mathrm{v}}C_{\mathrm{rec}})$. Swap refinement adds the number of evaluated remove/add exchanges times the same reconstruction cost. TRACE-SL therefore reports launcher and manifest artifacts rather than claiming polynomial-time optimal placement certification, submodularity, approximation ratios, or universally monotone empirical MAE.
\end{remark}
